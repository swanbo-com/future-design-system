MVP Platform Specification

future.ly

One story. One countdown. One community. A storytelling fundraising platform for grassroots action-sports projects where people follow the journey, support the project, and get something real in exchange.

Date: March 2026 Version: 1.0 — MVP Entity: UK CIC (registered) Confidential
🌊

One story at a time. Full focus. 30 days.

future.ly isn't a platform with many campaigns — it's one story at a time, with the full attention of the community. When a story is funded, it stays alive with updates. Then the next story takes its place. Sign up to see what's next. Unlike GoFundMe, people get something real. Unlike Kickstarter, the story never dies.

1. What we're building

future.ly is a platform where grassroots action-sports projects tell their stories and get funded through them. Every campaign centres on an exchange — not a donation. Funders contribute to a specific, transparent goal and receive something tangible and authentic from the community they supported.

The platform differentiates on three things that nobody else combines:

Living stories — Campaigns don't end when they're funded. Updates, photos, and progress reports keep the story alive. People follow along and stay connected to the communities they helped.

Tangible exchanges — Funders receive real things: handmade items, signed prints, personal thank-yous. Not donation receipts. Not digital badges. Something you'd hang on your wall.

Radical transparency — Every campaign has a public budget. Every dollar is accounted for. People know exactly what their money bought.

1b. The "One Story" model

future.ly doesn't launch as a marketplace of campaigns. It launches as one story. The entire site IS the campaign. This is a deliberate constraint that solves multiple problems at once.

Why one story is better than many

ProblemHow "One Story" solves it
Empty platform feelThere's no "browse campaigns" page with one entry. The site IS the story. It feels intentional, not sparse.
Attention dilutionOne story gets 100% of the community's focus, shares, and energy. No competition for eyeballs.
Quality controlWe curate intensely. Every story on future.ly is vetted, shot, and built by us. Quality is non-negotiable.
Urgency30-day countdown. When it's done, it's done. A new story takes its place.
Growth engine"Sign up to see the next story" converts visitors into a waitlist for Campaign One. Built-in lead generation.
Narrative arcThe platform itself has a story: "This was our first. Here's what happened. Now meet the next community."

The cycle

Story goes live → 30-day countdown → Community funds it → Story stays alive (updates) → Next story teased → Waitlist notified → New story goes live

What happens after 30 days?

If the campaign is fully funded: the exchange tiers close, the story transitions to "living story" mode (updates continue), and the homepage begins teasing the next story with a signup wall.

If the campaign is partially funded: the money raised still goes to the project (no all-or-nothing). The campaign page stays live with a "Funded: 72% of goal" badge. Exchange tiers remain open for an additional grace period (7 days). Then it transitions to living story mode regardless.

If the campaign fails to hit 30% in the first week: this is a kill signal. The story isn't reaching people. Pause, diagnose distribution, consider whether to extend or pull.

The 30/90 rule from Kickstarter data: campaigns that hit 30% of their goal in the first week have a 90% chance of fully funding. Track this metric from day one. If you're at 15% after 7 days, something is wrong with distribution, not the product.

1c. Engagement mechanics

These aren't gimmicks — they're behavioural design grounded in research. Every mechanic serves the story and the community. Nothing is fake, nothing is manipulative, everything is transparent.

The countdown (always visible)

A real 30-day countdown displayed prominently on the campaign page. This is a legitimate deadline — the campaign closes, period. No extensions, no "we'll keep it open." Research shows 20-30% of total funding on Kickstarter comes in the final 48 hours. The countdown creates a natural narrative arc: launch energy → mid-campaign engagement → final push urgency.

Design: large, beautiful, always visible in the sticky header on scroll. Days : Hours : Minutes. Not seconds — seconds feel like a sales timer. Days and hours feel like a deadline.

Live activity feed

A real-time ticker on the campaign page: "Sarah from Bristol backed the Standard tier — 3 min ago." Research shows funders are 50% more likely to back when they see peer activity. This creates herding behaviour — late-stage funders imitate early ones. Built on Supabase realtime subscriptions, so it's genuinely live, not batched.

Rules: first name + city only (privacy). Anonymous backers show as "Someone from London." No amounts shown in the feed (avoids wealth signalling). Feed shows last 10 entries, auto-scrolls.

Milestone progress bar

Not a simple fill bar — a narrative progress bar with four milestones tied to the campaign phases. Each milestone has a label describing what that funding level unlocks for the project:

MilestoneCampaign Zero exampleWhat happens
25% funded"Safety gear for all 15 kids"Content unlock #1 (behind-the-scenes video). Visual celebration on the progress bar.
50% funded"10 complete skateboard setups"Content unlock #2 (unseen photo set from Bren). Community wall highlight.
75% funded"Mini ramp materials purchased"Content unlock #3 (personal video message from the community). Push notification to all supporters.
100% funded"Full build: gear + ramp + skatepark plan"Content unlock #4 (the full story — trip footage, the build, the result). Celebration moment.

Content unlocks (the differentiator)

This is the mechanic that makes future.ly feel genuinely different. When the community collectively hits a funding milestone, new content is unlocked for everyone — supporters and visitors alike. It creates a sense of "we did this together" and gives people a reason to share: "We just unlocked a new video — help us get to 75%."

The content is pre-planned and loaded before launch. It's revealed, not created on the fly. Bren and the team plan four content pieces that map to the four milestones. The quality matters — each unlock should feel like a reward, not filler.

Content unlock planning (documentary footage — coordinate with Bren & Rick before launch):

25% unlock: Documentary teaser (2 min) — cinematic. The kids, the boards, the barangay. This is the trailer people share.

50% unlock: Extended raw scene — building a board from scrap wood. Unedited, intimate. Shows the craft and reality.

75% unlock: The kids speaking directly to the community. Subtitled. Their words, their faces, their thank-yous. Emotional peak.

100% unlock: Exclusive rough-cut scene from the documentary (5-10 min). A preview of the film that nobody else has seen. Creates anticipation for the full release.

Founding supporter status

The first 50 backers receive a "Founding Supporter" badge visible on the community wall. Numbered: "Founding Supporter #23." This creates early-bird urgency without discounting the exchange tiers. The badge is permanent — when Campaign One launches, founding supporters from Campaign Zero are visibly marked as originals. This is about identity, not perks.

Supporter count as social proof

"147 people are funding this story" — displayed prominently next to the progress bar. Not just the pound amount (which can feel abstract) but the number of humans. This is tribal psychology — people want to join a crowd that's already forming.

"Sign up for the next story" (growth engine)

Below the campaign, a simple CTA: "This is our first story. The next one is coming. Sign up to be the first to know." Email capture with a teaser of Campaign One (Our Ocean School — one image, one line). This converts every visitor into a lead for the next campaign, even if they don't fund this one.

What we deliberately don't do

MechanicWhy we skip it
Fake scarcity ("only 10 prints left!")Prints are unlimited. Lying about scarcity destroys the trust we're building. Premium boards have real scarcity — we don't need to manufacture it.
Auto-sharing to friendsNever post to someone's social media without explicit action. Social manipulation backfires and feels invasive.
Gamified donation amounts ("☕=£5")Patronising. Cheapens the exchange model. The tiers speak for themselves.
Streak mechanicsDoesn't fit a 30-day campaign. Streaks work for daily-use apps, not fundraising.
Leaderboards by amountCelebrating wealth, not generosity. A £10 supporter is as valuable as a £200 one.
Countdown in secondsSeconds feel like a Black Friday timer. Days and hours feel like a deadline.

3. Revenue model

future.ly uses a hybrid model designed to be the fairest in the space: a small fixed fee per exchange that covers platform costs, plus a dedicated Creator Fund that pays the photographers and videographers who tell these stories. No percentage-based fees that scale greedily with donation size. Every penny is accounted for publicly.

The model

ComponentAmountPurpose
Platform fee£2 flat per exchangeCovers hosting, development, operations, support. Does not scale with exchange size.
Creator Fund£1 per exchange + voluntary funder contributionsPays photographers, videographers, and content creators who tell these stories. Published transparently.
Payment processing~2.9% + 30p (Stripe)Unavoidable. Passed through at cost. Shown transparently.

Why this is different. Kickstarter takes 5% + 3-5% processing (effectively 8-10%). GoFundMe prompts a 13.5% default "tip." Patreon charges 10-15%. future.ly takes a flat £2 + £1 for creators, regardless of exchange size. On a £50 exchange, that's ~8.5% total (including processing). On a £200 auction, it drops to ~4.5%. The bigger the contribution, the more goes to the project.

Unit economics

On a £50 exchange: the project receives £45.25. Platform takes £2 (operations). Creator Fund receives £1. Stripe takes £1.75 (processing). On a £200 auction item: the project receives £191.50. The percentages improve as the exchange value rises.

Creator Fund transparency

The Creator Fund is published on the site. Every quarter: how much collected, who was paid, what for. Photographers and videographers receive a per-campaign fee (agreed upfront) for their work. This is the story of the storytellers being told transparently — and it's a differentiator nobody else offers.

Revenue streamModelPhase
Platform fee£2 flat per exchangeMVP
Creator Fund£1 per exchange + voluntary contributionsMVP
Payment processingPassed through at cost (~2.9% + 30p)MVP
Brand sponsorshipBrands sponsor campaigns (e.g. Vans sponsors skate projects)V2
Premium campaign toolsAnalytics, storytelling features for campaign creatorsV3

Tax note. HMRC treats exchanges as commercial sales, not donations. The CIC pays corporation tax on surpluses. VAT applies at 20% above the threshold (£90,000 as of 2026). Structure from day one: separate donation-only contributions (no VAT) from exchange purchases (VAT applicable). Consult accountant before launch.

Funder journey

Discovers story → Reads campaign → Chooses exchange tier → Pays via Lago/Stripe → Gets confirmation → Follows updates → Receives exchange

No account required to browse stories. Account required to fund (email + name). Returning funders can see all their supported campaigns in one place. Email notifications for story updates on campaigns they've funded.

Submits story → Review & vetting → Campaign built → Goes live → Posts updates → Receives funds

For MVP, campaigns are created by us (John + team). Campaign creator self-service is a Phase 2 feature. This keeps quality high and lets us develop a playbook before opening it up.

5. Exchange tiers

Every campaign has a tiered exchange model. The tiers scale from high-value scarce items to accessible micro-contributions. This is the Kickstarter reward model applied to grassroots impact.

TierWhat you getPrice modelSupply
PremiumOriginal handmade item (e.g. skateboard, artwork)Auction (starting bid ~$100)Scarce (5-10 per campaign)
StandardHigh-quality framed photo print from the story, signedFixed price ($25-$50)Unlimited
PersonalHandwritten thank-you card from the communityFixed price ($10-$15)Scales with community
SupporterName on the campaign wall + story updatesDonation (any amount, min $5)Unlimited

Fulfillment reality check. Physical exchanges require fulfillment infrastructure. For MVP: prints are produced by a UK-based print-on-demand partner (shortlist: Prodigi, Gelato). Handwritten cards are coordinated through on-the-ground contacts (Bren for Siargao). Premium auction items are shipped directly from origin — shipping cost is factored into auction reserve price, not absorbed by the platform. For boards specifically: sea freight PH→UK is ~37 working days, plus customs (12% duty + 20% VAT). Minimum viable auction price after costs: $200.

Content pipeline: documentary-driven

Campaign Zero is backed by a documentary shoot. This changes everything — instead of scrambling for content, the campaign draws from a professional production pipeline. The documentary shoot produces hours of footage; the platform becomes the documentary's companion experience. People follow the story on future.ly while the full film is being made.

The documentary is the content engine. This solves the biggest risk (stale content) because the production generates more material than the platform can use. The challenge flips from "how do we get enough content?" to "how do we curate the best moments?" Every future campaign should aim for this model — a documentary or serious content production backing the campaign, not phone footage and afterthoughts.

The documentary ↔ platform flywheel

The documentary and the platform feed each other:

  • The documentary produces content for the platform (daily footage, edits, scenes)
  • The platform funds the project the documentary covers (equipment, ramps, gear for the kids)
  • The platform audience becomes the documentary's built-in audience (screening invites, premiere access)
  • The documentary's release drives new traffic back to future.ly (for the next campaign)

This flywheel is the long-term model. Every future.ly campaign should ideally have a documentary or serious content production attached. The platform doesn't just fund projects — it funds the stories about them.

Minimum content commitment still applies. Even with documentary backing, campaigns must have a named content lead committed to weekly updates for the 30-day window. The documentary provides raw material; someone still needs to edit and publish platform-specific content. For Campaign Zero, that's Bren and Rick.

8. Campaign page anatomy

Each campaign page is the product. It needs to be beautiful, emotional, and functional. Here's the structure:

Above the fold

Hero image (full width, cinematic). Campaign title. One-line description. Funding progress bar with amount raised / goal. Number of supporters. Days remaining (if applicable).

The story

Photo-led narrative. Short paragraphs. Interspersed with full-width images and image grids. This is the emotional core — it should feel like reading a short documentary piece, not a fundraising appeal.

The exchange tiers

Sticky sidebar on desktop, card section on mobile. Each tier shows: what you get (photo), price, what the money funds, how many remaining (if limited). Clear CTA on each tier.

The budget

Collapsible section showing exactly where the money goes. Line items with amounts. This is the transparency mechanic — it builds trust.

The timeline

Reverse-chronological updates. Photos, text, video. Each update has a date and optional milestone badge. Funders get email notifications for new updates. This is the "living" part of the living story.

Community wall

Grid of funder names (or anonymous) with optional short messages. Social proof + belonging.

Campaign phases

Visual roadmap showing phases (e.g. Phase 1: Gear, Phase 2: Mini Ramp, Phase 3: Skatepark). Current phase highlighted. Completed phases marked. Creates a sense of journey and momentum.

9. Tech stack

Architecture overview

Two systems with clear separation of concerns. Strapi handles everything content (stories, updates, media, campaign narratives). Supabase handles everything transactional (auth, payments, orders, realtime feeds, auctions). Next.js sits in front of both.

LayerTechnologyRationale
FrameworkNext.js (App Router)From boilerplate. SSR for SEO. ISR for story pages. Fast shipping.
StylingTailwind CSSRapid UI development. Consistent design system.
CMSStrapi v5Headless CMS for all content: stories, updates, media galleries, campaign narratives, photographer profiles. Dynamic zones for flexible story layouts. Visual admin for non-technical creators. RBAC for admin/editor/photographer roles.
Transactional DBSupabase (Postgres)Auth, orders, payments, bids, realtime subscriptions (activity feed, auction countdown), waitlist. Everything that involves money or user state.
AuthSupabase AuthFunder auth: magic link (low friction) + social (Google). Strapi handles its own admin auth separately.
PaymentsLago + StripeLago for billing orchestration. Stripe for payment processing. Multi-currency handled by Stripe (display in local currency, settle in GBP).
Media CDNCloudinary or Cloudflare R2Image optimisation, responsive sizes, video transcoding. Strapi's media library uploads to Cloudinary via plugin.
EmailResendTransactional (receipts, update alerts, auction notifications) + waitlist notifications.
Video (critical)Mux or Cloudflare StreamPrimary content medium. Documentary footage requires adaptive streaming, HLS delivery, and thumbnail generation. Mux preferred — better API, built-in player, per-minute pricing ($0.007/min stored, $0.04/min delivered). Videos served inline on story pages, not behind embeds.
HostingDigitalOcean (full stack)Existing infrastructure. Strapi on DO App Platform or Droplet. Managed PostgreSQL for Strapi. Next.js on DO App Platform. All in one place.

10. Database schema

-- Campaigns
create table campaigns (
  id uuid primary key default gen_random_uuid(),
  slug text unique not null,
  title text not null,
  subtitle text,
  description text not null,          -- Rich text / markdown
  hero_image_url text,
  status text default 'draft',        -- draft, live, funded, completed, paused
  funding_goal_cents int not null,
  amount_raised_cents int default 0,
  currency text default 'GBP',
  supporter_count int default 0,
  starts_at timestamptz,
  ends_at timestamptz,                -- null = no deadline
  location text,                      -- e.g. "Siargao, Philippines"
  sport text,                         -- e.g. "skateboarding", "surfing"
  content_lead text,                  -- Name of person responsible for updates
  created_at timestamptz default now(),
  updated_at timestamptz default now()
);

-- Campaign phases (roadmap)
create table campaign_phases (
  id uuid primary key default gen_random_uuid(),
  campaign_id uuid references campaigns(id),
  title text not null,                -- e.g. "Phase 1: Proper setups and safety gear"
  description text,
  funding_target_cents int,
  status text default 'upcoming',     -- upcoming, active, funded, completed
  sort_order int default 0,
  created_at timestamptz default now()
);

-- Budget line items (transparency)
create table budget_items (
  id uuid primary key default gen_random_uuid(),
  campaign_id uuid references campaigns(id),
  phase_id uuid references campaign_phases(id),
  description text not null,          -- e.g. "10x complete skateboard setups"
  amount_cents int not null,
  spent_cents int default 0,
  category text,                      -- equipment, shipping, labor, materials
  sort_order int default 0
);

-- Exchange tiers
create table exchange_tiers (
  id uuid primary key default gen_random_uuid(),
  campaign_id uuid references campaigns(id),
  title text not null,                -- e.g. "Premium — Handmade Board"
  description text not null,
  image_url text,
  price_cents int,                    -- null for auction tiers

  min_donation_cents int,             -- for donation tier
  quantity_total int,                 -- null = unlimited
  quantity_remaining int,
  shipping_note text,                 -- e.g. "Ships worldwide. Allow 6-8 weeks."
  sort_order int default 0,
  is_active boolean default true,
  created_at timestamptz default now()
);

-- Auction items (for premium tier)

  id uuid primary key default gen_random_uuid(),

  image_urls text[],
  reserve_price_cents int not null,

  bid_count int default 0,

  ends_at timestamptz not null,

11. Payment architecture

Three payment types

TypeFlowImplementation
Fixed-price exchange Funder selects tier → Stripe Checkout → Lago invoice created → Confirmation email Standard Stripe Checkout session. Lago handles invoicing, receipts, and fee calculation.
Auction Funder places bid (no charge) → Auction ends → Winner receives Stripe payment link → Lago invoice Bids are stored in DB. No payment at bid time. Winner has 48h to pay. If unpaid, goes to next bidder.
Donation (supporter tier) Funder enters custom amount (min £5) → Stripe Checkout → Lago invoice → Name on wall Custom amount input. No physical exchange — digital acknowledgement only.

Fee breakdown per transaction

On every exchange, three deductions are made — all shown transparently at checkout:

FeeAmountWho receives
Platform fee£2 flatfuture.ly CIC (covers ops, hosting, development)
Creator Fund£1 flatPhotographers, videographers, content creators
Payment processing~2.9% + 30pStripe (at cost, not marked up)
Net to projectRemainder (~90%+ on larger exchanges)Campaign project (via Wise transfer, with CIC Regulator consent)

Multi-currency

Campaigns are denominated in a base currency (GBP for UK CIC). Funders pay in their local currency (Stripe handles conversion). Funds are disbursed to projects via Wise in the most efficient currency (e.g. PHP for Philippines, IDR for Indonesia). All disbursements to external project bodies require CIC Regulator consent.

VAT consideration. Exchange tiers where a physical item is received are treated as sales. Supporter tier (donation, no exchange) is not subject to VAT. The £2 platform fee and £1 creator fund contribution are service charges — VAT treatment to be confirmed with accountant. Register for VAT when total taxable turnover exceeds £90,000.

11b. Creator economics

Photographers and videographers shoot for free because they believe in the stories. When a campaign succeeds, they earn. This is itself a story worth telling — and it's published transparently on the platform.

Pure revenue share model

RoleShareWhen it pays
Lead photographer8% of net campaign revenueDistributed monthly, starting 30 days after campaign closes
Videographer5% of net campaign revenueSame schedule
Contributing photographer3% of net campaign revenueFor photographers who contribute updates (not the initial shoot)

On a campaign that raises £5,000 net (after platform fee and processing): the lead photographer earns £400, the videographer earns £250. On a campaign that raises £15,000: £1,200 and £750 respectively. The incentive is perfectly aligned — better content brings more funding brings more earnings.

Why this works

You have photographers willing to work for free who love the model. The revenue share turns "free work" into "invested work." They're not volunteers — they're equity holders in the story. They're incentivised to promote the campaign to their own audiences, keep creating content during the 30-day window, and contribute updates even after the campaign closes. This also means the content pipeline problem (who creates updates?) has a built-in answer: the people who earn from engagement.

Creator Fund mechanics

The £1-per-exchange Creator Fund still exists but its purpose shifts. Instead of directly paying creators (the revenue share does that), the fund covers:

  • Equipment and travel costs for photographers going to remote locations
  • Emergency support if a campaign underperforms (so creators aren't left with nothing)
  • New creator onboarding — covering costs for photographers on their first campaign before revenue share kicks in

The fund is still published transparently. Quarterly report: how much collected, what it was used for.

Creator profiles on the platform

Every photographer gets a visible profile on the campaigns they shoot. Their name, photo, and story appear alongside the community they're documenting. When someone funds a campaign, they're supporting the photographer too — and that's made explicit. "Shot by Bren — who left his media career to document grassroots communities across Southeast Asia. He earns a share of every exchange on this campaign."

11d. The personal connection

This is the soul of future.ly. If someone funds a campaign and feels like they donated to a website, we've failed. They should feel like they know the kids, know the photographer, and have been to the barangay themselves. Here's how we build that feeling.

Know the people

Every campaign has a "Meet the people" section — not a generic "about the community" paragraph, but individual profiles with faces, names (with consent), and one-line stories.

PersonWhat's shownConsent requirement
The kids (Campaign Zero)First name, age, photo, one sentence ("Kael, 11 — rides a piece of plywood down a hill and calls it heaven")Written parental consent (bilingual). No surnames. No exact location beyond barangay.
The photographerFull name, photo, their story ("Bren left his media career to document grassroots communities across SE Asia"), revenue share disclosureStandard consent. They're an adult choosing to be public.
The on-ground teamName, role, photo, connection to the communityStandard consent.

Design principle: when someone scrolls through the "Meet the people" section, they should be able to close their eyes and picture the community. Not as a concept — as specific humans. Kael rides plywood. Bren quit his job. Rick carries a camera everywhere. This specificity is what turns a donation into a relationship.

Two-way connection

Funders aren't spectators — they're woven into the story.

  • Messages at checkout. Every funder can leave a message when they back the campaign. These appear on the community wall.
  • Funder questions woven into updates. The content team reads funder messages. The best questions and comments get addressed in story updates: "Maria from London asked if the kids have helmets yet — here's the answer" with a photo of helmets arriving. This makes funders characters in the story.
  • Direct thank-yous. The personal tier includes a handwritten thank-you card. But beyond that: video updates should occasionally address the community directly. "To the 147 people who made this possible — here's what your money bought this week."

For MVP, this is editorial, not automated. No chat system, no comment threads (too moderation-heavy). The content team reads messages and weaves them into updates manually. This keeps the quality high and the voice consistent.

Feel like you're there

This is about content format and design decisions that create immersion.

  • Full-width photography. No thumbnails. No grid of tiny images. Each photo is full-width, cinematic, given room to breathe. The design gets out of the way.
  • Inline video clips. Short clips (10-30 seconds) that autoplay muted on scroll — the sound of wheels on concrete, kids laughing, waves. Not behind a YouTube embed. Native, embedded, seamless.
  • Ambient audio option. A small play button in the corner that plays ambient sound from the location while you read. Optional, not intrusive. The barangay comes alive.
  • No stock anything. Every image, every video, every sound is from the actual community. This is the authenticity promise.
  • Scroll-driven narrative. The story unfolds as you scroll — inspired by NYT/Guardian multimedia longform. New sections reveal as you move down. It's a reading experience, not a fundraising page.

Content priority for MVP: video-first. You're shooting a documentary — the platform should feel like it. The main story is video-led with supporting photography. Inline clips (15-60s) play as you scroll through the narrative. Full video pieces anchor the content unlocks. Photography fills the gaps and works for social sharing, email, and the community wall. The design should handle video natively — not as YouTube embeds, but as first-class media with adaptive streaming (Mux or Cloudflare Stream).

12. Safeguarding policy

This is a launch blocker. No campaign featuring children goes live without all of these completed.

Before any content goes public

  1. Written parental consent Bilingual consent forms (English + local language) walked through with each family in person. Covers: use of images/video on the platform, use in social media promotion, right to withdraw consent. Forms signed, scanned, stored securely.
  2. Safeguarding policy document Published on the site at /safeguarding. Covers: how we work with children, who's responsible, how to report concerns, data handling for minors. Based on UK Safeguarding framework.
  3. Content review process All images/video featuring children are reviewed before publication. No content that could be considered exploitative, sexualising, or endangering. No full names of children published without explicit parental consent. Location specificity limited (barangay level, not home addresses).
  4. DBS-equivalent checks All adults working directly with children undergo background checks. UK: DBS. Philippines: NBI clearance. Indonesia: SKCK (Police clearance).
  5. Philippines compliance SEC registration check for foreign entity working with children. DSWD clearance for child welfare activities. Compliance with RA 7610 (child protection) and RA 9775 (anti-child exploitation) for all published content.
  6. UK Online Safety Act compliance Platform risk assessment completed. Age-appropriate content guidelines published. Content moderation process documented. Reporting mechanism for concerns.

Non-negotiable. If consent forms aren't completed before the Siargao trip, no content from the trip goes on the platform. Period. This protects the kids, the families, and the CIC.

14. Campaign quality control

A bad campaign kills trust for every other campaign on the platform. Quality control is how future.ly stays credible.

MVP: Curated by us

For MVP and V1, all campaigns are sourced and built by the future.ly team. No self-service campaign creation. This means every campaign has:

  • A verified story (we've been there or have a trusted on-ground contact)
  • A named content lead committed to weekly updates
  • A realistic, priced-out budget
  • Exchange items that can actually be fulfilled
  • Complete safeguarding compliance (if children involved)

V2: Submission + vetting

When we open to external campaigns, the process will be:

  1. Submission Project submits story, photos, budget, team info, and proposed exchanges via /submit form.
  2. Review future.ly team reviews for authenticity, feasibility, and content quality. Video call with project lead.
  3. Campaign build If approved, future.ly works with the project to build their campaign page. We handle design and storytelling structure.
  4. Content commitment Project lead signs a content commitment agreement: weekly updates for the first 3 months, bi-weekly thereafter.
  5. Launch Campaign goes live. future.ly monitors for the first 30 days and provides storytelling support.

Kill criteria for campaigns. A campaign is paused or removed if: no updates for 14+ days without explanation, funds are demonstrably misused, safeguarding concerns are raised, or the project team becomes unresponsive. Funders are notified and offered the choice of a refund or reallocation to another campaign.

15. Risks and mitigations

RiskSeverityMitigation
Platform feels empty with 1-2 campaigns Solved The "One Story" model makes this a feature, not a bug. The homepage IS the campaign. One story, full focus, 30-day countdown. No browse page, no empty grid. The next story waitlist converts visitors into leads. Scarcity is the design.
Story updates go stale High Content commitment agreement for every campaign. Minimum weekly photo update. Automated email reminders to content leads. Platform dashboard shows "days since last update" prominently.
Shipping economics don't work for premium items Medium Price shipping into auction reserve. Offer local pickup / event collection option. For standard prints, use global POD (ships from nearest facility). Don't promise worldwide free shipping on handmade items.
VAT/tax treatment is wrong High Get professional accountant advice before launch. Separate exchange sales (VATable) from donations (not VATable) in the payment system from day one. Don't retrofit.
Safeguarding incident Critical Complete safeguarding policy before launch. Written consent for every child. No content without review. Incident response plan documented. Named safeguarding officer.
Campaign funds misused High Funds held by CIC, not project directly. Disbursed against budget line items with receipts, with CIC Regulator consent for all transfers to external bodies. Public budget shows spend vs. plan. If misuse detected, campaign paused and funders notified.
Payment processor / banking failure Medium Stripe as primary + Wise as backup for direct transfers. Don't depend on a single banking partner (Mainvest lesson). CIC bank account with Wise provides multi-currency resilience.
All funders are within personal network Medium This is a kill signal. Track "degrees of separation" — if 80%+ of funders are known contacts after 30 days, the story isn't reaching strangers. Invest in distribution (Vice feature, Bren's audience, surf/skate media) before launch.

Green signals

  • Day 7: Hit 30% funded (triggers the 30/90 rule — 90% chance of full funding)

  • Day 30: 50+ funded exchanges total
  • Day 30: Board auction bids exceed $200
  • Day 30: 100+ email signups for "next story" waitlist
  • Post-campaign: A third grassroots project approaches inbound
  • Post-campaign: 20%+ of Campaign Zero funders also support Campaign One

Kill signals

  • Day 7: Below 15% funded — distribution isn't working
  • Day 7: 80%+ of funders are within 2 degrees of personal network
  • Day 14: Haven't hit 25% (first content unlock) — momentum is dead
  • Day 30: Fewer than 20 total exchanges
  • Day 30: Page views high but conversion below 2% — story resonates but exchange model doesn't
  • Any time: Shipping costs for boards exceed 50% of auction value
  • Pre-launch: Safeguarding compliance can't be completed
  • Post-launch: No story update published for 14+ days

18. Design direction

future.ly is not Kickstarter green or GoFundMe blue. It's warm, natural, photographic. The brand feels like a National Geographic documentary meets a zine. Premium but accessible. Serious about impact but not somber.

Visual principles

  • Photo-led. The images carry the emotion. UI gets out of the way.
  • Warm neutrals. Off-white backgrounds, warm grays. Not clinical white, not dark mode.
  • Teal accent. #2A9D8F from the Siargao validation doc. Ocean, nature, trust.
  • Generous whitespace. Let the content breathe. One idea per scroll.
  • Typography-first. Inter or similar clean sans-serif. Small body text, generous spacing. Headers don't need to shout.
  • No stock photos. Every image on the platform is real, from the actual projects.

Tone of voice

  • Direct. No charity-speak. No "make a difference today." No guilt.
  • Authentic. Speak like the communities we represent. Real language.
  • Confident. We know these stories matter. We don't apologise for asking.
  • Warm. Not corporate. Not performative. Human.

What we never do

  • Poverty porn. No sad-face-kid-looking-into-camera shots.
  • Guilt-driven copy. No "for the price of a coffee."
  • Fake urgency. No countdown timers on donations (auctions are the exception).
  • Corporate charity language. No "impact metrics" or "beneficiary outcomes."

21. Legal structure

Entity: UK Community Interest Company (CIC). Asset lock ensures funds stay tied to social mission. Allows trading (critical for exchange model). Social enterprise credibility.

CIC Regulator consent for fund transfers. Because future.ly raises funds and transfers them to external project bodies (e.g. community organisations, local partners), the CIC Regulator must consent to these transfers. This is standard for CICs whose ordinary activities include moving funds to other organisations. The CIC36 community interest statement must include the phrase "with the consent of the CIC Regulator" wherever it describes fundraising and disbursement to projects. All fund transfers to project bodies require Regulator consent — build this approval step into the disbursement workflow.

Banking: Wise Business Account. Multi-currency (GBP, USD, PHP, IDR). Real exchange rates for international transfers. Payment links for manual invoice handling.

Platform terms: Clear terms of service covering: exchange is a purchase (not a donation for tax purposes), fulfillment timelines, refund policy (full refund if exchange not delivered within stated timeframe), data handling (GDPR compliant), content licensing (projects grant future.ly license to display content).

Refund policy: Full refund if exchange is not fulfilled within the stated timeframe. Partial refund option if project is paused or cancelled — funder chooses between refund or reallocation to another campaign. Auction items: no refund after payment (standard auction terms).
