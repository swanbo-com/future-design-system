In a barangay on the southern tip of Siargao, fifteen kids ride skateboards made from scrap wood, bottle caps, and flip-flop rubber. They bomb hills on the main highway, barefoot, laughing, dodging tricycles.

Nobody taught them. There's no skatepark, no shop, no YouTube tutorial that works on their connection speed. They watched older kids surf and thought: we want that feeling too. So they built boards from whatever they could find — driftwood, old planks, bent nails for axles.

The boards barely roll. Some don't turn at all. But the kids ride them every day after school, down the same hill, over and over, until the sun drops behind the palms and their parents call them home.

Handmade boards — scrap wood, bent nails, bottle-cap bearings

They don't need saving. They need setups.

This isn't a charity story. These kids aren't victims — they're builders, inventors, skaters who happen to live somewhere where a real skateboard costs more than a month's family income. They've already proven they'll ride anything. We just want to see what they do with the real thing.

The plan is specific: 15 complete setups (decks, trucks, wheels, bearings, helmets, pads), materials to build a proper mini ramp, and a community session led by local riders. Every peso is accounted for publicly. The budget is below.

Bren has been documenting this community for two years. He didn't set out to make a project — he just kept coming back because the kids kept building new boards. When strangers started messaging him wanting to help, we built future.ly to give them a way to do it that actually works.
