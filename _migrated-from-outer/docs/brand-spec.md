## 1. Discovery

### What future.ly is

A documentary-style fundraising platform where real stories fund real projects. One project at a time. A community of supporters, creators, filmmakers, and the people at the heart of the story — all connected through a living campaign that updates over time.

Run as a UK CIC. Every pound accounted for.

### Who it's for

Three layers of community:

**The people in the story** — the primary community. The kids in Siargao building skateboards. The surfers in Cornwall. The communities these projects serve. They're not recipients — they're the protagonists.

**The creators** — photographers, videographers, editors, community organisers who tell the stories and run the projects. They're not employees — they earn a share of what the campaign raises.

**The supporters** — people who fund, follow, and share the story. They're not donors — they're participants. They get something tangible back. They follow what happens. They're part of it.

### The problem we solve

Crowdfunding platforms treat stories as pitches. The campaign ends and the story dies. Funders feel like ATMs. Grassroots projects in remote communities don't have slick marketing teams — they have incredible stories that nobody is telling properly. And no platform gives those stories the treatment they deserve.

### How it should feel

When someone lands on future.ly, they should feel like they've walked into a documentary screening at a friend's place. Not a charity event, not a tech launch, not a shop. Something real is happening, it's beautifully presented, and they can be part of it.

The feeling is: *I found something genuine. I want to follow this.*

### The cringe list — what future.ly is NOT

- **Not charity guilt.** No sad faces, no "just £5 can change a life", no rescue framing.
- **Not startup tech-bro.** No disrupting, scaling, hustling, growth-hacking for good.
- **Not corporate NGO.** No stock photos, no mission statements, no annual reports in blue and white.
- **Not generic.** Not boring. Not something that looks like every other fundraising platform.

## 3. Positioning

### Positioning Statement

For people who care about grassroots projects and want more than a donation receipt, future.ly is a documentary fundraising platform that tells one story at a time — with updates that keep going, exchanges that give you something real, and full transparency on where the money goes. Unlike Kickstarter, the story lives on. Unlike GoFundMe, you're a participant, not a donor.

### Brand Pillars

| Pillar | What it means | How it shows up |
|--------|---------------|-----------------|
| **Community first** | The people in the story are the centre. The supporters, creators, and filmmakers form the community around them. Everyone is connected. | Activity feed. Community wall. Creator profiles. Supporter badges. Updates that feel like messages from friends, not marketing emails. |
| **One story, full focus** | One campaign at a time. When it's done, the next one begins. No browsing, no comparing, no algorithm. | Single campaign architecture. Countdown timer. Waitlist for the next story. The site IS the story. |
| **Show, don't tell** | The story does the convincing. We never beg, guilt, or hard-sell. The documentary content — short, visual, punchy — is the pitch. | Short paragraphs. Big images. Inline video. The content speaks for itself. |
| **Everything visible** | CIC structure. Public budgets. Creator revenue shares. Platform fees explained. You see exactly where your money goes. | Fee breakdown at checkout. Budget page on every campaign. Creator Fund explained publicly. |

### Value Proposition

- **Primary:** Follow real stories from the ground and fund them into reality — with something tangible to show for it.
- **Secondary:** One story at a time. Documentary-quality content in short, visual blocks. Full transparency. Direct creator support.
- **Emotional:** I'm not just watching or donating. I'm part of this story. I backed it early. I have the proof on my wall.

## 4. Personality

### The documentary filmmaker

If future.ly were a person, they'd be the one at the party who just got back from somewhere incredible. Tanned, relaxed, tells amazing stories without showing off. Has a camera bag but isn't precious about gear. Buys the first round. Everyone gravitates toward them.

They don't lecture. They don't pitch. They show you something and let you decide if you care. And you always care, because the story is real and they tell it well.

### Attributes

| Attribute | It means | It doesn't mean |
|-----------|----------|-----------------|
| **Grounded** | Real places, real people, real stories. No polish that erases reality. We show the dirt on the road and the tape on the skateboard. | Depressing, poverty-porn, exploitative. We show reality with dignity, not to make people feel guilty. |
| **Cinematic** | Everything looks and feels considered. The photography has intention. The video is edited with care. The page has rhythm. | Overproduced or slow. We're not a Netflix trailer. Think handheld camera with incredible natural light, told in short punchy blocks you can scroll through quickly. |
| **Clean** | Whitespace. Breathing room. Nothing competes with the content. The platform gets out of the way. | Sterile, cold, minimal-for-the-sake-of-it. Clean means warm and inviting, not empty. Think Product Hunt's ease, not a brutalist art gallery. |
| **Welcoming** | Anyone can be part of this. The language is simple. The design is accessible. You don't need to know about crowdfunding or action sports to feel at home. | Childish, dumbed-down, or performatively casual. Welcoming means clear and friendly, with respect for the audience's intelligence. |
| **Real** | No marketing speak. No hype. If we say something, it's true and specific. Numbers, names, places. The opposite of vague inspirational platitudes. | Blunt or cold. Real means honest and specific, not harsh. We're the friend who tells you the truth, not the one who tells you what you want to hear. |

## 5. Voice & Tone

### Voice (constant)

future.ly always sounds like this, regardless of context:

```
Formal ←————————[——●——]———→ Casual
    Leans casual. A person, not a platform.

Reserved ←———[——●——]————————→ Enthusiastic
    Measured. Lets the story do the exciting.

Minimal ←———————[——●——]———→ Expressive
    Says enough to land, then stops.

Serious ←———[——●——]————————→ Playful
    Warm, not heavy. Knows when to smile.

Technical ←————————————[——●——]→ Simple
    Plain language always. A 16-year-old skater and a
    40-year-old photographer both feel at home.
```

### Tone (adapts by context)

| Context | Tone | Example |
|---------|------|---------|
| Campaign story | Documentary narrator — calm, observational, lets images lead | "They started with a plank of coconut wood and a set of borrowed wheels." |
| Exchange tiers | Direct, clear, no hard sell | "Limited-edition darkroom print. Numbered and signed. Ships worldwide." |
| Countdown / progress | Factual, not panicked | "14 days left. 63% funded. 112 people in." |
| Funder updates | Warm, insider, like a message from location | "Quick update from Siargao — the kids finished their first board today." |
| Errors | Honest, brief | "Payment didn't go through. Check your card and try again." |
| Success | Genuine, grounded | "Funded. Every penny accounted for. The build starts Monday." |
| Social media | Visual-first, minimal caption | Let the image or clip do the work. Caption is one line. |
| Waitlist | Anticipation without hype | "One story at a time. Leave your email. You'll be first to know." |
| Transparency pages | Honest, detailed, clear | "We're a UK CIC. Here's exactly where your money goes." |

### Writing Principles

1. **Show, don't pitch.** The story makes people want to fund. We never beg, guilt, or hard-sell.
2. **Short and punchy.** Short sentences. Short paragraphs. Real words. If a 14-year-old in Siargao wouldn't understand it, rewrite it.
3. **Present tense, active voice.** "They build boards from scrap wood" — not "Boards were built by the participants."
4. **Let the image lead.** Copy supports photography and video, never competes. When the visual is strong, one sentence is enough.
5. **Specifics over generalities.** "127 people funded this in 9 days" — not "Our amazing community came together."
6. **Respect, not rescue.** These are communities with agency. We never frame anyone as a victim or an object of pity.

### What we never say

- **"Donate"** — It's an exchange, a contribution, or backing.
- **"Help these people"** — Rescue framing. They're building something. We're supporting it.
- **"Make a difference"** — Empty. Show the specific difference instead.
- **"Revolutionary" / "disrupting" / "game-changing"** — We're not a pitch deck.
- **"Amazing" / "incredible" / "inspiring"** — If the story is good, you don't need to say it's inspiring.
- **"Just £5 can…"** — No guilt-pricing. The exchange tiers speak for themselves.
- **"Our community"** possessively — It's not ours. It's the community around this story.
- **"Empower"** — Overused to meaninglessness. Say what actually happens.
- **"100% goes to the project"** — It doesn't and shouldn't. We're transparent about fees.

## 6. Visual Identity

*Revised April 2026 to reflect the v1.2 wireframe direction and Campaign Zero product line.*

### Design Philosophy

**The platform disappears. The content is the design.**

future.ly is a warm editorial frame for documentary photography and video. The UI stays back. The images and clips carry all the colour, energy, and emotion. When you're scrolling a campaign, you should barely notice the platform — you're in the story.

"Minimal" means warm, tactile, and inviting — generous whitespace, natural materials palette, easy to read, easy to share. The feeling is an editorial magazine spread about a community you want to join, not a dark cinema screening and not a tech product page. The cream-and-ink foundation lets full-bleed photography carry the visual intensity, while the page itself feels calm and trustworthy.

### Colour Palette

#### Core (Campaign Pages — Primary Context)

| Role | Colour | Hex | Usage |
|------|--------|-----|-------|
| **Background** | Warm cream | `#F5F1EA` | Primary background. Campaign pages, editorial scroll, checkout. Natural, paper-like. |
| **Paper / Surface** | Off-white | `#FFFDF8` | Cards, elevated surfaces, the progress card, tier cards. Subtle lift from background. |
| **Ink** | Near-black | `#111111` | Headlines, body text, key data. Warm-dark, not blue-black. |
| **Mute** | Stone | `#6B645A` | Captions, metadata, timestamps, secondary info. |
| **Rule** | Sand | `#CFC4AF` | Borders, dividers, milestone markers. |
| **Vermillion accent** | Warm vermillion | `#D93A2B` | Primary accent. Progress fill, CTAs, links, funded %, merch print colour. The one colour that pops — surf-poster red, Endless Summer energy. |
| **Vermillion dark** | Deep red | `#B22D21` | Hover states, inline accent text, milestone-unlocked glyph. |
| **Warning** | Amber | `#D97706` | Time-sensitive states, "current milestone" tags. Sits naturally next to vermillion without clashing. |

#### Accent Variants

| Role | Colour | Hex | Usage |
|------|--------|-----|-------|
| **Vermillion light** | Soft red | `#E5524A` | Hover states, active indicators |
| **Vermillion muted** | Dusty red | `#A03020` | Tag backgrounds, selected states, subtle emphasis |
| **Vermillion wash** | Red at 8% | `rgba(217,58,43,0.08)` | Highlight areas, education row on the ledger, queued-milestone background |

#### Semantic

| Role | Colour | Hex | Usage |
|------|--------|-----|-------|
| **Success** | Muted green | `#059669` | Milestone unlocked, funded confirmation |
| **Warning** | Amber | `#D97706` | Time running low, pre-order state |
| **Error** | Warm red | `#DC2626` | Payment failures, form errors |
| **Cut / removed** | Document red | `#8B1A10` | Internal wireframe annotations only — not user-facing |

#### Dark Mode (secondary contexts)

Used for: the post-purchase upsell modal, video player chrome, and the "keep future.ly running?" subscription prompt. Dark inverts the palette to create a visual break at the conversion moment.

| Role | Colour | Hex |
|------|--------|-----|
| Background | Near-black | `#111111` |
| Surface | Charcoal | `#1A1A1A` |
| Text | Warm white | `#FAFAF9` |
| Accent text | Pale vermillion | `#F28C83` |
| Accent CTA | Warm vermillion | `#D93A2B` |

#### Physical / Merch Palette

The merch and packaging palette is even simpler than the digital one. Two colours. No gradients.

| Context | Base | Ink | Notes |
|---------|------|-----|-------|
| **T-shirt** | Vermillion `#D93A2B` (fabric) | White (print) | One-colour screen print. White on red. |
| **Print insert card** | Natural kraft (uncoated) | Black ink | Single colour, no red. The card is quiet — the print is the hero. |
| **Print mailer** | Natural kraft | Black stamp or sticker | `future.ly` wordmark only. |
| **Stickers / press kit** | White vinyl | Vermillion `#D93A2B` | For fridge, laptop, notebook, skate-shop counter. |

#### Why this palette?

- **Warm cream because editorial trust.** This is a commerce site asking for money. Warm cream says "newspaper of record" — trustworthy, legible, approachable. The photography carries all the visual intensity through full-bleed image blocks; the frame around it just feels calmer.
- **Vermillion because surf-poster heritage.** The Endless Summer. Morning of the Earth. Red connects to the classic surf-documentary poster tradition without being GoFundMe green, Kickstarter green, or charity-blue. It's warm, bold, and immediately distinct. On a white tee or a cream background, vermillion is the only colour that needs to exist.
- **Ink-and-paper because transparency.** The "every receipt on the table" promise reads better on a warm surface than a dark void. The transparency ledger feels like a real accounting document, not a UI widget.
- **One accent, one warning, that's it.** Vermillion for the brand, the progress, the energy. Amber for the things that need attention. Everything else is ink on paper.
- **Two colours on merch. Always.** Red tee, white ink. White sticker, red ink. Kraft card, black ink. Never more than two. The constraint is the aesthetic — it's what makes a Lobster Shanty tee or an Endless Summer poster feel timeless rather than designed-by-committee.

### Typography

| Role | Font | Weight | Rationale |
|------|------|--------|-----------|
| **Display / Headlines** | **Fraunces** (optical size 9–144) | 300, 400 (italic), 600, 800 | Display serif with warmth and character. The italic swashes carry editorial personality without being precious. Works at very large sizes (clamp 48–104px for hero) down to card headers (22px). |
| **Body / UI** | **Instrument Sans** | 400, 500, 600 | Clean, modern, excellent legibility at body sizes. Neutral enough to not compete with Fraunces or the photography. Replaces Inter — more contemporary, slightly warmer x-height. |
| **Data / Annotations** | **JetBrains Mono** | 400, 500 | Budget breakdowns, transparency ledger, milestone amounts, countdown timers, progress stats. Gives a "raw data / audit trail" feeling that supports the transparency promise. Also used for section labels and wireframe annotations. |

#### Type Scale (1.25 ratio, revised)

- Display: clamp(48px, 7vw, 104px) — campaign title (Fraunces 300, line-height 0.92)
- H1: 40px — section headers (Fraunces 300)
- H2: 28px — tier prices, milestone amounts (Fraunces 300 or 600)
- H3: 22px — card headers, milestone names (Fraunces 600)
- Body: 15px / 18px desktop — narrative text (Instrument Sans 400)
- Small: 13px — captions, descriptions (Instrument Sans 400)
- Mono-label: 10–11px uppercase, letter-spacing 0.12em — section labels, tags (JetBrains Mono 400/500)
- Micro: 9px uppercase, letter-spacing 0.12em — tier kickers, milestone state tags (JetBrains Mono)

Body line-height: 1.55. Max content width: 640px for text blocks. Full-bleed for images and video.

### Spacing & Layout

- **Base unit:** 4px
- **Border radius:** 2px. Sharp, editorial. Not rounded — rounded says "app," sharp says "print."
- **Box shadow signature:** Offset solid shadow (e.g. `10px 10px 0 #111`) on key cards (progress card, hero tier). This is the "editorial blueprint" visual signature — it makes cards feel like physical objects pinned to a board.
- **Whitespace philosophy:** Generous. More space than feels necessary. Content breathes. 88px between major sections. 40px between story beats. If it feels too spacious in design, it's probably right in production.
- **Full-bleed content:** Images break out to card-width with 1px ink border. The diagonal-pinstripe pattern fills image placeholders during pre-launch.
- **Vertical rhythm:** 88px between major sections. 28px within blocks.
- **Two-column layout:** Main content (fluid) + annotations column (280–300px, sticky) on desktop. Single column under 900px. The annotations column is for internal/wireframe use — it doesn't ship to production but sets the precedent for how we annotate design decisions.

### The Cascade as a Visual Element

The four-step milestone cascade is not just a funding mechanic — it's a core brand element. It appears on the campaign page, in social assets, in the pitch deck, and in press materials.

**Visual rules for the cascade:**
- **One bar, four markers.** Never two bars. Never a pie chart. Never a percentage circle. The cascade is a horizontal ladder.
- **Hatched fill.** The filled portion of the bar uses a 45-degree hatched pattern in vermillion (`repeating-linear-gradient(45deg, #D93A2B 0 8px, #B22D21 8px 16px)`). This makes it visually distinctive and avoids the "generic progress bar" look.
- **Milestone markers are vertical ticks** on the bar, not dots or icons. Clean, architectural.
- **Education milestone (#3) gets a pencil glyph** (✎) after its name — a small visual cue that it's a different kind of milestone (classroom, not concrete) without breaking it out of the cascade.
- **States:** Unlocked (✓, vermillion-dark text), Current (●, vermillion text), Next (plain, muted text), Stretch (plain, muted text).
- **In social/press:** the cascade can be rendered as a standalone horizontal strip — a "campaign ruler" that becomes a recognisable visual shorthand for the project.

### Photography

The most important visual element. future.ly IS the photography.

- **Documentary, not stock.** Shot on location, by the creators, during the project. No staged shots.
- **Natural light.** Golden hour, harsh midday, overcast — whatever's real. No studio lighting.
- **Full-bleed priority.** Key images use full card width with a 1px ink border. The image IS the design.
- **Human scale.** People in context. A kid skating with mountains behind. A builder in a workshop with light through bamboo. The environment is part of the story.
- **Unpolished is fine.** Motion blur, grain, dust, sweat. If it looks like a real moment, it's the right photo.
- **Respectful framing.** Communities shown with dignity and agency. No poverty-porn. People are doing things, building things, living life.
- **Aspect ratios vary.** 4:5 portrait for story beats, 5:4 landscape for location context, 1:1 for community close-ups, 16:9 for video. Variety is intentional — it creates the editorial scroll rhythm.

### Iconography

- Lucide icons (rounded, 1.5px stroke)
- Used sparingly — icons support text, never replace it
- No decorative icons, no emoji as design elements
- Exception: the ✎ pencil glyph on the education milestone — this is a typographic mark, not an icon

## 6c. Physical Product & Packaging

The Cebu-finish hero unit is the one physical object that 1,000 backers will hold. It needs to feel like a real product from a brand that cares, not a padded envelope from a warehouse. This is where the brand becomes tangible.

### Hero Unit (Tier 2 — Darkroom Print)

**Components (assembled in Cebu before shipment to ShipBob):**

1. **A3 archival print** — giclée on Hahnemühle or equivalent acid-free stock. This is Bren's work — it's the hero.
2. **Acid-free tissue sleeve** — clear or translucent. Protects the print. No branding on the sleeve itself.
3. **Stiffener board** — white or natural kraft, same dimensions as the print. Prevents bending.
4. **Insert card** (see below).
5. **Kraft mailer** — rigid, 350gsm+ kraft. Flat ship format per dev spec §26b. Exterior label only (ShipBob affixes routing label; Cebu self-ship gets a hand-stamped return address).

### Insert Card

The insert card is the brand's voice inside the package. One card, two sides.

**Front:**
- `future.ly` wordmark top-left (Fraunces italic, small)
- Campaign name: "Campaign Zero — Siargao Skate Kids" (Instrument Sans, small caps or JetBrains Mono uppercase)
- One-line: "This print funded [current milestone] in [location]." — dynamically determined at pack time based on where the cascade was when the order was placed, but for Campaign Zero's first run, a single static line is fine: *"This print is funding safety gear, a mini ramp, a school classroom, and a skate park in Siargao, Philippines."*
- QR code linking to `future.ly/zero/transparency` (the public ledger)
- Printed on uncoated natural kraft card stock, black ink only

**Back:**
- "Shot by Bren Lopez. Siargao, 2026."
- Print title / location line if applicable
- "future.ly is a UK CIC. Every pound accounted for."
- Small: `futurly.com · @futurly`

**Design principles for the card:**
- One colour (black ink on natural kraft). No teal, no gradients. The physical object earns its warmth from the material, not from print effects.
- Typography matches the digital brand: Fraunces for the wordmark, JetBrains Mono for the metadata, Instrument Sans for the body line.
- Size: 5×7 inches or A6. Fits inside the sleeve with the print.

### Auction Board Packaging (Tier 1 — Handmade Skateboard)

- Wrapped in unbleached tissue or kraft paper.
- A hand-written note from the kid who made/painted it (facilitated by Bren, with parental consent). This is the ultimate human touch — no printed card replaces it.
- Shipped from Cebu directly (not via ShipBob — these are signed, one-of-a-kind, with tracking + signature required). See dev spec §26b routing for `requires_signature_at_source`.
- Outer packaging: a custom-length cardboard tube or flat rigid mailer depending on dimensions. Branded with a single `future.ly` stamp or sticker — don't over-brand a handmade object.

### General Packaging Principles

- **Materials > branding.** Kraft, tissue, uncoated card. The materials communicate quality and care. Don't plaster logos everywhere.
- **One brand mark per package.** The insert card carries the detail. The exterior gets one small wordmark or stamp. That's enough.
- **The print is the hero.** Everything in the package exists to protect and frame the print. Nothing competes with Bren's photograph.
- **Sustainability by default.** All packaging recyclable. No plastic tape (use paper tape). No foam inserts. No unnecessary filler. If it doesn't protect the print, it doesn't go in the box.

## 9. What We Never Do

### Visual

- Stock photography. If we don't have real photos, we don't launch.
- Gradient backgrounds.
- Avatar grids. People appear as full portraits in context.
- Product-grid layouts for exchange tiers. They emerge from the narrative.
- Decorative illustration. No blob people, no abstract shapes.
- Dark patterns. No fake urgency, no guilt modals, no hidden fees.

### Language

- Everything in the "what we never say" list
- Corporate jargon: leverage, synergy, ecosystem, stakeholder
- Startup speak: disrupt, pivot, scale, hustle
- Charity clichés: make a difference, give back, change lives
- Superlatives without evidence: best, most, first, only, unique
- Emoji in professional copy (campaign pages, emails, about page)

### Behaviour

- Hiding fees. All costs visible before checkout.
- Guilt-based conversion. If someone doesn't fund, that's fine.
- Exploiting subjects. Informed consent for every person in every photo. Guardian consent for minors. No exceptions.
- Fake scarcity. If a print run is 50, it's 50. If the deadline is 30 days, it's 30 days.

### Campaign page
Dark mode. Teal accent on progress/CTAs. Full-bleed photography. Short text blocks at 640px width. Generous whitespace. The page should feel like scrolling through a short documentary, not reading a crowdfunding pitch.

### Exchange checkout
Clean. Transparent. Show the fee breakdown before payment. No surprises.

Can be light or dark. Minimal. One field, one sentence, one photo.

Light mode. The CIC story. Budget diagrams. Team photos. Honest language.

One column. Photo at top. Short text. Feels like a message from someone on location.

Visual-first. Minimal caption. Let the image or clip carry it. Platform brand stays subtle.

## 11. One-Liner Candidates

The brand spec has had "TBD" here since March. Here are four candidates to pick from or remix. The one-liner should work on the homepage, in the meta description, on social bios, and in press kits.

**Option A — Story-forward:**
> *"One story. One community. Every receipt on the table."*
>
> Why: leads with the single-campaign focus, ends with the transparency promise. Three short beats. Easy to remember.

**Option B — Action-forward:**
> *"Fund the story. Follow the money. Keep the proof."*
>
> Why: three verbs, three steps, mirrors the backer journey (pledge → watch the ledger → receive your print). Punchier, more commerce-coded.

**Option C — Descriptive (for press/context):**
> *"Documentary-driven commerce for grassroots communities."*
>
> Why: the most literal description. Works as a subtitle, a press kit lead, or the "what we do" line in investor decks. Not emotional — functional.

**Option D — Emotional / warm:**
> *"Real stories, real exchanges, real receipts."*
>
> Why: the repetition of "real" reinforces the anti-bullshit positioning. Warm but grounded. The "receipts" at the end signals transparency with a colloquial edge.

**Recommendation:** A for the homepage hero, C for press materials, B or D for social bios. They're not mutually exclusive — they serve different contexts. But if forced to pick one that goes everywhere, **A** is the safest bet.
